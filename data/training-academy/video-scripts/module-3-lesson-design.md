### [02:00] BACKWARD DESIGN - START WITH THE OBJECTIVE

Here's the first mistake new instructors make: They start with an activity. "I want students to build a game" or "I want to teach them about persuasive writing."

That's not an objective. That's a topic.

*[VISUAL: Diagram showing "Topic" vs "Objective"]*

An objective is a *measurable outcome*. It answers the question: "What will students be able to do by the end of this lesson that they couldn't do before?"

Here's the difference:

**Topic:** "Students will learn about character development."
**Objective:** "Students will create one character with a backstory, motivation, and weakness that drives their narrative."

See how the second one is specific, observable, and measurable? You can look at a student's work and know whether they hit the objective. The first one? You have no idea what success looks like.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "What will students DO, not just KNOW?"]*

This is called *backward design*. You start with the end goal, then build the lesson structure to get there.

Checkpoint 1 asks you to draft an objective-aligned agenda. Here's how:

**Step 1:** Write one clear objective. Use this format: "By the end of today's lesson, students will be able to [specific, observable action]."

**Step 2:** Identify the success criteria. What does good work look like? What are the non-negotiables?

**Step 3:** Build the sequence backward. What do students need to practice to hit the objective? What do they need to see modeled first? What prior knowledge do you need to activate?

*[VISUAL: Backward design flowchart - Objective → Success criteria → Sequence]*

When you start with the objective, every part of your lesson has a purpose. You're not just filling time—you're building toward something.

### [04:00] THE GRADUAL RELEASE MODEL

Now let's talk about the best-practice sequence for skill-building lessons. It's called the *gradual release of responsibility*, and it has four phases.

*[VISUAL: Four-step graphic - "I Do, We Do, You Do Together, You Do Alone"]*

**Phase 1: I Do (Model)**
You demonstrate the skill while students watch and listen. You think aloud, show your process, and name the key steps. This is not lecture—this is modeling.

Example: "Watch me write the first paragraph of this persuasive essay. I'm starting with a hook—a surprising statistic. Then I'll state my claim clearly. See how I'm doing that?"

**Phase 2: We Do (Guided Practice)**
Now students try the skill *with you*. You're still leading, but they're actively involved. You might do think-pair-share, call on students to suggest next steps, or co-create an example together.

Example: "Okay, now let's write the second paragraph together. Turn and talk: What evidence should we include? I'll give you one minute."

**Phase 3: You Do Together (Collaborative Practice)**
Students work in pairs or small groups to practice the skill. You circulate, listen, and provide just-in-time support. This is where misconceptions surface—and where you catch them.

Example: "With your partner, write your own hook and claim. You have eight minutes. I'll be walking around."

**Phase 4: You Do Alone (Independent Practice)**
Students apply the skill independently. You're still available for questions, but they're driving their own work now.

Example: "Now write the rest of your essay independently. Use the rubric to self-check as you go."

*[VISUAL: Timeline showing how much time to spend in each phase - I Do 10%, We Do 20%, You Do Together 30%, You Do Alone 40%]*

This sequence works because it scaffolds learning. You don't throw students into the deep end. You model, you guide, you release gradually.

And here's the key: If your formative checks during "You Do Together" show confusion, *you loop back*. You don't just keep marching forward. That's how you end up with the 45-minute disaster from the opening.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Model → Guide → Release"]*

### [06:00] TRANSITIONS - THE HIDDEN TIME THIEF

Let's talk about something most new teachers don't plan: *transitions*.

The shift from whole-group to partner work. The moment you ask students to pull up a new document. The thirty seconds it takes to distribute materials.

If you don't plan transitions, they eat your lesson alive.

*[VISUAL: Clock graphic showing wasted time adding up - 2 min + 3 min + 2 min + 4 min = 11 minutes lost]*

Think about it: If you lose two minutes per transition and you have five transitions in a lesson, that's ten minutes gone. Ten minutes you could have spent on guided practice or closure.

Here's how you fix it.

**Strategy 1: Clear Cue**
Signal the transition with a consistent phrase or sound. "In thirty seconds, you'll turn to your partner." "Three, two, one—eyes on me." "Computers closed, materials out."

**Strategy 2: Purpose Statement**
Tell students *why* you're transitioning. "We're moving to partner work because I want you to practice this skill with support before you try it alone."

**Strategy 3: Explicit Next Action**
Tell students exactly what to do. Not "Get ready for the next activity." Say: "Open the shared doc, find your partner's name, and start reading their draft."

*[VISUAL: Three-part transition formula - Cue + Purpose + Action]*

Checkpoint 2 asks you to plan transition language for every phase of your lesson. Here's an example:

> "Alright, we've finished modeling. Now we're going to try this together. In ten seconds, turn to your partner and share one idea for your character's motivation. Go."

That transition took twelve seconds and got everyone moving. Compare that to:

> "Okay, um, so now we're going to do pair work. Find a partner, I guess. Talk about your character or whatever."

That transition takes two minutes and half the class isn't sure what they're doing.

*[PAUSE 2 SEC]*

Tight transitions are the mark of an experienced teacher. Start building that habit now.

### [08:00] FORMATIVE CHECKS - YOUR REAL-TIME FEEDBACK LOOP

Here's the most important lesson design skill you'll learn: *checking for understanding in the moment*.

You can't wait until the end of class to find out students are confused. By then, it's too late. You need formative checks every 10-15 minutes.

*[VISUAL: Lesson timeline with checkpoints marked every 10-15 minutes]*

Checkpoint 3 asks you to include at least three checkpoints to measure understanding mid-lesson. Here are six techniques:

**1. Thumbs Check**
"Thumbs up if you're confident, sideways if you're unsure, down if you're stuck." Fast, visual, gives you instant data.

**2. Think-Pair-Share**
"Think for thirty seconds: What's the main cause of the conflict? Now turn and tell your partner. I'll call on someone in one minute." Everyone practices before you cold-call.

**3. Exit Ticket (Mid-Lesson)**
"On a sticky note, write one question you still have. Put it on the board as you leave for the bathroom break." You read them during the break and adjust.

**4. Show Me**
"Everyone open your doc and show me your thesis statement. Hold your screen up." You scan the room and see who's on track.

**5. Cold Call with Scaffolding**
"I'm going to call on someone in thirty seconds to explain Step 2. Talk to your neighbor first." The wait time reduces anxiety and improves answers.

**6. Fist to Five**
"Show me on your fingers: How confident are you that you could explain this to someone else? Five is very confident, fist is not at all." You see the distribution instantly.

*[VISUAL: All six techniques listed with icons]*

Here's the crucial part: *You have to act on what you learn.*

If 60% of thumbs are down, you don't keep going. You reteach. You say: "I see a lot of uncertainty. Let me show you one more example, then we'll try it again."

That's what separates good teachers from great ones. Great teachers adjust in real time based on student signals.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Check, adjust, continue. Repeat."]*

### [10:00] WHEN TIME RUNS SHORT - THE TRIAGE DECISION

Real talk: You will run out of time. A discussion will go long. A student will ask a great question that derails your plan. The tech will break. It happens.

The question is: What do you cut?

*[VISUAL: Lesson plan with sections labeled "Core," "Important," and "Nice to Have"]*

Here's the priority framework:

**Protect First: Objective-Aligned Practice**
If students don't practice the skill, they won't learn it. Cut everything else before you cut the "You Do Together" or "You Do Alone" phase.

**Cut Second: Non-Essential Discussion**
That five-minute tangent about a student's cool side project? Save it for the end if there's time. It's great for culture, but it's not core to today's objective.

**Cut Third: Extensions**
Planned a bonus challenge for fast finishers? If you're short on time, let them use those extra minutes to refine their core work instead.

**Protect Last: Closure**
Never skip closure entirely. Even if it's just ninety seconds, you need to anchor the learning and set up next steps.

*[VISUAL: Priority pyramid - Objective-aligned practice at base, closure at top]*

Checkpoint 3 includes thinking about this. When you build your agenda, label each component: *Core*, *Important*, or *Nice to Have*. That way, when you're in minute thirty-five and running behind, you already know what to cut.

Here's a pro move: Build in *flex time*. Plan forty minutes of content for a fifty-minute class. Use the extra ten minutes for deeper discussion if things go smoothly, or as a buffer if they don't.

You're not a robot. Lessons won't run like clockwork every time. But you can design for flexibility.

### [12:00] EXTENSION AND SCAFFOLDING - DESIGNING FOR MIXED READINESS

Your students won't all work at the same pace. Some will finish in twenty minutes. Some will need forty. If you don't plan for that, you'll have bored students and frustrated students in the same room.

Checkpoint 4 asks you to prepare one extension and one scaffold for mixed readiness levels. Here's how.

*[VISUAL: Two-column chart - "Extension" and "Scaffold"]*

**Extension: For students who finish early**
This is *not* busywork. It's not "Do more of the same." It's a task that deepens mastery by adding complexity.

Example: Your objective is "Create a character with backstory, motivation, and weakness." A student finishes in fifteen minutes.

❌ Bad extension: "Make another character."
✅ Good extension: "Add an internal conflict to your character—something they want that contradicts what they need. Write how this will complicate their story."

The extension is directly tied to the objective, but it pushes deeper.

**Scaffold: For students who are stuck**
This is a simplified entry point that still leads to the same objective.

Example: A student stares at a blank page. They can't think of a character at all.

❌ Bad scaffold: "Just try harder."
✅ Good scaffold: "Choose one of these three character archetypes: the reluctant hero, the comic relief, or the misunderstood villain. Pick one and I'll give you a template to start."

The scaffold reduces choice, provides structure, but still asks the student to do the thinking.

*[VISUAL: Examples of both shown side-by-side]*

When you design extensions and scaffolds, you're saying: *This objective is for everyone, but the path to get there can be different.*

That's differentiation. And it's non-negotiable in YPP classrooms where students come in with wildly different skill levels.

### [14:00] CLOSURE - THE MOST SKIPPED, MOST IMPORTANT PHASE

Let's talk about the part of the lesson most teachers skip when they run out of time: closure.

And that's a huge mistake.

*[VISUAL: Image of students packing up while teacher is still talking]*

Closure is not "Okay, see you next time!" Closure is a structured reflection that consolidates learning and sets up the next step.

Checkpoint 5 asks you to create a 3-minute closure that captures reflection and next step. Here's the formula:

**1. Recap the Objective (30 seconds)**
"Today's goal was to create a character with backstory, motivation, and weakness. Look at your doc—did you include all three?"

**2. Evidence-Based Reflection (90 seconds)**
Ask a specific reflection question tied to the objective:
- "What was the hardest part of developing your character's weakness? Type your answer in the chat."
- "Turn to your partner: What's one thing you learned today about character development that you didn't know before?"
- "Fist to five: How confident are you that you could create another character using this process?"

**3. Clear Next Action (60 seconds)**
Tell students exactly what happens next:
- "By next class, I want you to add two more paragraphs to your character's backstory. Use the model we looked at today."
- "Tonight, read chapter three and come ready to discuss how the author uses character motivation."
- "Your draft is due Friday. Before you leave, check the rubric and identify which criterion needs the most work."

*[VISUAL: Three-part closure formula - Recap + Reflect + Next Action]*

This three-minute investment does three things:
1. It reinforces what students learned so they don't forget it by tomorrow.
2. It gives you formative data (those reflection responses tell you what stuck and what didn't).
3. It creates continuity—students leave knowing what's next, not wondering what just happened.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Never skip closure. Ever."]*

When you're running short on time, trim the middle of the lesson—not the closure. A rushed lesson with a strong close is better than a complete lesson that just... ends.

### [14:30] APPLICATION BRIDGE - FROM VIDEO TO CHECKPOINTS

Alright, let's connect this to your five checkpoints.

*[VISUAL: Flowchart showing how checkpoints build on each other]*

**Checkpoint 1** asks for an objective-aligned agenda. Start with one measurable objective. Build your sequence using the "I Do, We Do, You Do Together, You Do Alone" model. Label each section with a time estimate and mark what's core vs. nice-to-have.

**Checkpoint 2** is your transition language. Write out the exact words you'll use to shift between phases. Practice saying them out loud. Aim for transitions that take less than sixty seconds.

**Checkpoint 3** is your formative checks. Choose three techniques from the list I gave you and write when you'll use them. Minute ten: thumbs check. Minute twenty-five: think-pair-share. Minute forty: exit ticket. Be specific.

**Checkpoint 4** is extension and scaffold. Design one task for students who finish early (add complexity, don't just add quantity). Design one simplified entry point for students who are stuck.

**Checkpoint 5** is your closure prompt. Use the three-part formula: recap, reflection question, next action. Write it out word-for-word so you're not improvising when the bell is about to ring.

*[PAUSE 2 SEC]*

These aren't theoretical. These are the artifacts you'll use in your first real lesson. Build them like blueprints.

### [15:00] CLOSE - THE ARCHITECTURE OF LEARNING

Here's the truth about lesson design: It's not creative genius. It's architecture.

You're building a structure that supports learning. You need a strong foundation (clear objective). You need load-bearing walls (guided practice). You need a roof (closure). And you need to check the foundation every few minutes to make sure it's holding (formative checks).

*[VISUAL: Metaphor graphic - lesson plan as a building blueprint]*

When your lesson falls apart, it's usually not because you're a bad teacher. It's because the structure wasn't sound.

You skipped the model phase and students didn't know what to do. You didn't plan transitions and lost ten minutes. You didn't check for understanding and half the class was lost.

The good news? Structure is a skill. You can learn it. You can practice it. And you can get really, really good at it.

*[ON-SCREEN TEXT: "Great lessons are designed, not improvised"]*

So go build your first agenda. Write those transition scripts. Plan those formative checks. Because when you walk into your first YPP class, you won't be winging it. You'll have a blueprint.

And that blueprint will hold.

*[PAUSE 2 SEC]*

Welcome to lesson design. Now go build something great.
